Why I Started Zampa

The moment I realized I needed to create Zampa wasn't in a classroom or at a conference. It was in a small school in the highlands, watching students' faces light up with possibility.

As a Tibetan-American, I've always felt caught between worlds. In America, I had every material comfort but struggled with mental health and peer pressure. In India with my family, I saw harsh conditions but genuine happiness and community.

During my most recent trip to Hanle and Samey, something clicked. I was teaching English to eager students, showing them glimpses of the wider world. Later, my cousin asked me to teach her the dranyen. In those moments, I felt a sense of purpose I'd never experienced back home.

I realized that the bridge between these two worlds wasn't just for me – it could help others too. Young people in the highlands deserve access to education and opportunity. Young people in America deserve connection to tradition and purpose. And the challenges we face – climate change, mental health, disconnection – require insights from both worlds.

"Zampa means 'bridge' in Tibetan. It's not just a metaphor – it's a commitment."

A commitment to creating real connections across cultures, to sharing models of purposeful living, to showing that true change comes from understanding and engaging with different ways of being.

This is a lifetime commitment for me. As I embark on my university journey, I'm more convinced than ever that we need these bridges. And I'm dedicated to building them, one connection at a time.
